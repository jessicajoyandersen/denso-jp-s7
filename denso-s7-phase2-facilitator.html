Block A
Review Blitz — S1–S6
A fast oral review of everything learned so far. Facilitator-driven — call out a situation, learners respond. The quick reference grid on their screen is the cheat sheet they shouldn't need.
⏱
Suggested time
5–6 min
Fast and oral — this is recall, not re-teaching
💬 Talking Points
›
Open by naming the range: "We're going to do a quick pass through everything — S1 through S6. I'll give you a situation, you give me the phrase. No looking things up."
›
Use the quick reference grid on their screen as a fallback if someone gets stuck — the goal is to identify gaps, not to punish them. If no one gets it, that's a note for you.
›
Hit the high-frequency phrases: ohayou gozaimasu, otsukaresama, osaki ni, mou ichido, yoroshiku, ja nai desu, kore/sore/are, kono/sono/ano. That's the core.
›
Keep the pace brisk. If a phrase comes out fast and clean, move on. If someone hesitates, pause and have the group say it together once — then move on.
👁 Watch For
💡
If most learners are solid, keep this block short — 4 minutes is fine. The time is better spent on new content. If gaps appear, slow down on that phrase only, then pick back up.
⚠️
The gokurousama/otsukaresama hierarchy trap still trips people. If it comes up, one quick reminder: otsukaresama goes in every direction; gokurousama only flows downward. Then move on.
🔗 Block B — の Possessive

2 / 5
Block B
の — The Possessive Connector
One rule: の connects two nouns and says the first one owns or defines the second. Teach the rule once, then drill with floor vocabulary.
⏱
Suggested time
6–7 min
The S6 connection is the key insight — use it
💬 Talking Points
›
Write the rule on the board: [Noun A] の [Noun B] = Noun B that belongs to Noun A. Say it with a floor example: "Yamada-san の paatsu = Yamada-san's part. A-rain の tejun = A-line's procedure."
›
The S6 connection is the key insight — say it explicitly: "You already used の in Session 6. Kono, sono, ano, dono are ko/so/a/do + の fused together. When you said kono paatsu, you were already using の." This reframes new content as prior knowledge.
›
Flag the one watch-out: このの / そのの does not exist. Kono already has の built in. You never add another の after kono/sono/ano.
›
Drill the question form: Kore wa dare no ~ desu ka? (whose ~ is this?) and the short answer: Watashi no desu. Point out that you can drop the noun when both people can see what's being discussed — the の carries it.
›
Run the back-chain drill together — it's on their learner screen. Learners tap ▶ Start Drill to open it.
👁 Watch For
⚠️
このの is the main error. Learners sometimes try to add の after kono/sono/ano. Correct it once clearly — kono already contains の, so adding another is redundant.
💡
If learners get the pattern quickly, have them generate their own examples: their name + no + a floor noun. "Tanaka-san no kikai" etc. Producing their own examples is stronger than repeating yours.
💬 Block C — が好きです
Block C · 7–8 min · likes/dislikes + sentence builder
3 / 5
Block C
が好きです — Likes & Dislikes
The pattern that makes communication personal. Heaviest new content in Phase 2 — budget time for the particle explanation and the sentence builder.
⏱
Suggested time
7–8 min
The が particle is the sticking point — spend the time here
💬 Talking Points
›
Write the pattern: [Noun] が 好きです。 Say it out loud with examples: "Futtobooru ga suki desu. Yakyuu ga suki desu. Kono shigoto ga suki desu." Let them hear the rhythm before they try it.
›
Spend a moment on the particle: "It's が, not は. With suki desu, が is always the particle — it spotlights the thing being liked. Don't swap it out. Memorise the chunk: [thing] が suki desu." You don't need to explain why — just lock in the pattern.
›
Show both forms side by side: ~ ga suki desu (I like ~) and ~ ga suki ja nai desu (I don't like ~). Point out that suki ja nai desu uses the same ja nai desu from Session 6 — one more instance of prior knowledge.
›
Point out the two question forms on their screen. Supootsu ga suki desu ka? = "Do you like sports?" — yes/no. Suki na supootsu wa nan desu ka? = "What sport do you like?" — assumes they do, asks which. The second is warmer and more natural as a conversation opener. If a Japanese colleague asks it, they're already assuming common ground.
›
Run the back-chain drill together. Then give them 2–3 minutes with the sentence builder — learners tap ▶ Open Builder first. The builder now has categories (Sports, Music, Food, Other) with 20 options. Ask one or two people to share what they built and say it aloud.
›
The "me too" response: Watashi mo suki desu. Demonstrate a mini-exchange: you say "Futtobooru ga suki desu, Tanaka-san wa?" and have a learner respond with either their own preference or "watashi mo suki desu."
👁 Watch For
⚠️
は for が. The most common error. If you hear "futtobooru wa suki desu" — correct it once, clearly: が is the fixed particle with suki. Don't explain the full grammar; just redirect to the chunk.
😄
You don't need to ask if someone likes food. Tabemono ga suki desu ka? ("Do you like food?") is technically correct but basically meaningless — we all like food. Skip the yes/no and go straight to Suki na tabemono wa nan desu ka? (What food do you like?). Worth a laugh if it comes up in the builder.
💡
If learners get the back-chain quickly, push to the exchange: "Tell your partner one thing you like, then ask them — Tanaka-san wa? / [Name]-san wa?" A 60-second pair practice is worth more than another drill repetition.
💡
The sentence builder only shows 🔊 on natural sentences. If someone asks why they can't hear one, that's the point — it's not floor-ready. Use it as a teachable moment.
☀️ Block D — Social Ice Breakers
Block D · 5 min · ii otenki desu ne / sou desu ne
4 / 5
Block D
Social Ice Breakers — いいお天気ですね
Light content, high cultural value. The goal is one insight and one exchange. Don't over-drill — let the cultural explanation do the work.
⏱
Suggested time
5 min
One exchange, one insight — keep it light
💬 Talking Points
›
Lead with the comparison: "In English we say 'how are you?' and we don't actually expect an honest answer. It's a social signal — 'I see you, I'm friendly.' Japanese has the same thing, and the weather is the most common vehicle for it."
›
Important: sequence matters. Ohayou gozaimasu opens — that's the required greeting. Ii otenki desu ne comes after, as a warm add-on. Their colleague responds sou desu ne. That's the full sequence: greeting → observation → agreement → move on.
›
Explain ね: "The ne at the end is like adding 'right?' or 'isn't it?' — you're sharing an observation and inviting agreement, not asking a real question. Your colleague knows the expected move."
›
Run through the variants: ii otenki desu ne (nice weather), samui desu ne (cold today), atsui desu ne (hot today). Three phrases covers most days of the year.
›
Practice the full sequence as a group: ohayou gozaimasu → ii otenki desu ne → sou desu ne. Do it twice — once you say the opener, once a learner does. Four seconds total. That's the point.
👁 Watch For
💡
Some learners will want to ask "but what if the weather is actually bad?" — that's the right question. Samui desu ne and atsui desu ne work for those days. The point isn't the weather; it's the ritual. Any benign shared observation does the job.
⚠️
Don't let this block run long. It's intentionally light. If discussion opens up around Japanese small talk culture, give it 60 seconds then move to Block E.
🏭 Block E — Floor Exchanges

Block E
Floor Exchanges — Everything Together
Full 2–3 turn exchanges combining の, が好きです, ice breakers, and S1–S6 phrases. Read aloud with a partner, then discuss. Closes Phase 2.
⏱
Suggested time
5–6 min
Keep it light — this bridges into Phase 3
💬 Talking Points
›
Have pairs read the exchanges out loud — not just read silently. Even a quiet voice counts. The physical act of speaking the phrase is the point.
›
Point out where the new patterns land: "See exchange 1 — 'watashi no desu' with の. Exchange 3 — the full ice breaker sequence. Exchange 4 — が好きです in a real conversation."
›
Close Phase 2 with: "Phase 3 starts with cue cards — you'll use today's new patterns plus everything from S1–S6. Then 10 floor scenarios. You've got the vocabulary. Now you practice reading the situation."
👁 Watch For
💡
Some pairs will rush through silently. Encourage speaking aloud — even a murmur counts. Muscle memory of the phrase is the goal.
💡
If time is short, it's fine to skip the exchanges and just review the quick reference grid. The grid is on their screen and they can use it in Phase 3.
✅ Closing — Parting Words & Homework
›
Closing questions to ask the group (pick 2–3):
· Which phrase from today would you most likely use this week on the floor?
· Is there a situation at work where you wished you had the Japanese for something?
· What felt solid? What still feels shaky?
· Did the weather feel like the weirdest thing we taught today — and does it make sense now why it matters?
›
Parting words: "You came in knowing phrases. You're leaving knowing grammar — の, が好きです. That's a real shift. The phrases were vocabulary. Today was structure. Those are different things and you should feel good about it."
›
Low-stakes homework (no submission): Before the next session, notice one moment this week where you could have used の or が好きです — with a Japanese colleague or even just in your head. You don't have to say it out loud. Just notice the moment. That's it.
